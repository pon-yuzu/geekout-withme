多文化の食

A Nation Built on Diverse Flavours

Australia's culinary landscape is one that could not have existed without the successive waves of immigration that have shaped the country over the past century. What began as a predominantly British food culture, characterised by meat pies and roast dinners, has been transformed into one of the most diverse dining scenes on earth. The post-war influx of immigrants from Southern Europe introduced Australians to dishes they had never previously encountered — pasta, souvlaki, and espresso became staples virtually overnight. Subsequent waves of migration from Southeast Asia, the Middle East, and the Indian subcontinent further enriched the national palate. Were one to stroll through any major Australian city today, they would discover Thai, Lebanese, Vietnamese, Ethiopian, and Japanese restaurants coexisting harmoniously within a single neighbourhood. This multicultural approach to food, which many sociologists argue reflects Australia's broader commitment to inclusivity, has produced a cuisine that defies simple categorisation. It is this very diversity that Australians take the greatest pride in.

3 Review
📍 Laksa King — Melbourne
⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

Had someone told me a year ago that the best laksa I would ever taste could be found in suburban Melbourne, I might not have believed them. Yet Laksa King, nestled in the unassuming suburb of Flemington, serves a bowl so authentic that Malaysian visitors have reportedly declared it superior to many establishments in Kuala Lumpur. The curry laksa, prepared using a recipe that is said to have remained unchanged for over two decades, delivers a complexity of flavour that left me genuinely speechless. The broth, having been simmered for hours with a proprietary blend of spices, achieves a depth that mass-produced alternatives could never replicate. Were the restaurant located in a more central area, I suspect the queues would stretch around the block. The owner, whom I had the pleasure of speaking with briefly, explained that she wished more Australians understood the regional variations within Malaysian cuisine. Seldom does a humble restaurant leave such an indelible impression.

5 🦘 Australia Tips

多文化オーストラリアの食の語彙

オーストラリアでは cuisine（料理文化）、fusion（融合料理）、authentic（本格的な）などの語彙が日常的に使われます。"What cuisine are you in the mood for?"（何料理の気分？）はレストラン選びでよく聞くフレーズです。

関係代名詞 whom の使い方

"The owner, whom I had the pleasure of speaking with" のように whom は目的格の関係代名詞です。口語では who で代用されることも多いですが、準2級レベルでは whom の正しい使い方を理解しておくことが重要です。前置詞 + whom の形にも注意しましょう。

6 Conversation
🎭 多文化な食のエリアを友達と歩きながら、どこで食べるか相談する
A (Koa):
This street is incredible! There must be at least twenty different cuisines represented here.
B:
That's what I love about this neighbourhood. It's as if the whole world had gathered in one place.
A (Koa):
If you could only choose one restaurant tonight, which would it be?
B:
That's a tough question! I've been told that the Vietnamese place on the corner serves the most authentic pho in the city.
A (Koa):
Really? Who told you that?
B:
A colleague of mine, whose parents emigrated from Hanoi, said she wished she could eat there every day.
A (Koa):
That's quite an endorsement! But I also noticed a Lebanese restaurant that looked amazing.
B:
You mean the one with the outdoor seating? Had I known you were interested in Lebanese food, I would have suggested it immediately.
A (Koa):
Let's compromise. What if we had pho tonight and saved the Lebanese place for next week?
B:
That sounds like a perfect plan. Were we to try a different restaurant each week, it would take us months to get through this street.
A (Koa):
I wouldn't mind that at all! It would be like travelling the world without leaving Melbourne.
B:
Exactly! That's precisely what makes Australian food culture so remarkable.
