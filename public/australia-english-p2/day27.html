Navigating the Share House Market in Australia

Securing affordable accommodation in Australia's major cities, particularly Sydney and Melbourne, has become increasingly challenging for Working Holiday makers. The share house, a living arrangement in which multiple tenants occupy a single property, each renting an individual room while sharing communal areas, represents the most economically viable option for the majority of international visitors. Were one to search for a private apartment in the inner city, the monthly rent would likely exceed two thousand dollars, a figure that most budget-conscious travellers would consider prohibitive. Platforms such as Flatmates.com.au and Facebook Marketplace have emerged as the primary resources through which prospective tenants can browse available rooms, filtering by suburb, price range, and household preferences. It is strongly recommended that anyone considering a share house conduct a thorough inspection prior to committing, paying particular attention to the condition of communal facilities, the clarity of house rules, and the compatibility of existing housemates. Having identified a suitable property, the tenant is typically required to pay a bond equivalent to four weeks' rent, in addition to two weeks' rent in advance. This bond, which functions as a security deposit, is held in trust and returned upon departure, provided that no damage has been sustained.

📍 Sunny Share House, Newtown, Sydney
⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

Having viewed no fewer than eight share houses across Sydney over a period of two weeks, I can confidently say that this Newtown property surpassed all others in every respect. The moment I walked through the door, I was struck by the warmth with which the existing housemates, three of whom were also international travellers, welcomed me. The house, a beautifully maintained terrace built in the early twentieth century, features a spacious communal kitchen equipped with everything one could possibly need, a living room furnished with comfortable sofas and a large television, and a private courtyard where barbecues are regularly held. Were I asked to identify a single shortcoming, I might mention that the bathroom is shared among four people, which occasionally requires patience during morning routines. However, this minor inconvenience is far outweighed by the advantages. The bond, which amounted to 800 dollars, was registered with Fair Trading, a government body that ensures tenants' deposits are protected. Had I not discovered this place through a recommendation from a fellow backpacker, I would probably still be searching.

5 🦘 Australia Tips

内見で聞くべき質問リスト

シェアハウスの内見では次の質問が重要です。'Is the bond registered with Fair Trading?' (ボンドは公正取引局に登録されていますか？)、'What are the house rules regarding guests?' (ゲストに関するハウスルールは？)、'Are utilities included in the rent?' (光熱費は家賃に含まれますか？) これらを事前に準備しておくと安心です。

関係代名詞の応用：前置詞 + which/whom

'The house in which I live...' や 'The housemates, three of whom were international...' のように、前置詞＋関係代名詞はフォーマルな表現です。日常会話では 'The house I live in' と前置詞を後置しますが、書き言葉では前置が好まれます。

🎭 シェアハウスの内見で現在の住人と話す場面
A (Koa):
Come on in! You must be the person who enquired about the room. I'm Sam, one of the housemates.
B:
Nice to meet you, Sam. Thanks for having me over. The place looks wonderful from the outside.
A (Koa):
Cheers! So, this is the room that's become available. It's fully furnished — bed, desk, wardrobe, and a bedside lamp. Were you to need anything else, there's extra furniture stored in the garage.
B:
It's a lovely room. Could I ask about the house rules? I'd like to know what's expected of tenants.
A (Koa):
Of course. We ask that everyone keep the communal areas tidy, which isn't difficult provided that each person cleans up after themselves. We've also established a cleaning roster for the bathroom and kitchen.
B:
That sounds perfectly reasonable. What about overnight guests? Would it be permitted to have a friend stay occasionally?
A (Koa):
Absolutely, on condition that you let the other housemates know in advance. We're quite relaxed about it, honestly.
B:
Great. And regarding the financial side — how much is the bond and what's included in the weekly rent?
A (Koa):
The bond is four weeks' rent, so that's 1,200 dollars. The weekly rent of 300 includes water and internet, though electricity is split equally among all tenants.
B:
I see. Is the bond registered with Fair Trading?
A (Koa):
It certainly is. We insisted that the landlord register it properly. Had we not done so, there would be no legal protection for any of us.
B:
That's very reassuring. I'm really impressed with everything I've seen. I'd love to move in, if the room is still available.
