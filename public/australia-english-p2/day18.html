The Rise of Asian Cuisine in Australia

Over the past three decades, Asian cuisine has undergone a remarkable transformation in Australia, shifting from what was once considered exotic to becoming an integral part of the national food identity. This evolution, driven primarily by immigration from countries such as China, Vietnam, Japan, and Thailand, has resulted in a dining landscape where authentic Asian restaurants are as commonplace as traditional pubs. Japanese food, in particular, has experienced an extraordinary surge in popularity. Ramen shops, many of which are operated by Japanese-born chefs who relocated to Australia seeking new opportunities, have proliferated across major cities. It is reported that Melbourne alone has over two hundred dedicated ramen establishments, a figure that would have seemed inconceivable merely a decade ago. What distinguishes the Australian approach to Asian food is the willingness to preserve authenticity while simultaneously embracing local adaptations. Were one to sample ramen in Sydney's Chinatown, they would encounter broths as meticulously crafted as those found in Tokyo's most celebrated establishments.

3 Review
📍 Hakata Gensuke — Melbourne
⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

Were I to be asked which ramen restaurant in Melbourne most closely replicates the experience of dining in Fukuoka, my answer would be Hakata Gensuke without a moment's hesitation. Established by a Japanese chef who is said to have spent years perfecting his tonkotsu recipe before emigrating, this restaurant serves a broth so rich and deeply flavoured that it defies description. The noodles, imported directly from a supplier in Kyushu with whom the owner has maintained a longstanding relationship, possess exactly the right firmness that discerning ramen enthusiasts demand. What I found particularly noteworthy was the adherence to traditional preparation methods — the broth, having been simmered continuously for eighteen hours, develops a velvety texture that no shortcut could achieve. Had the restaurant not enforced a strict no-substitution policy for its signature tonkotsu, the consistency of quality might have suffered. Rarely does one encounter such unwavering dedication to culinary perfection outside of Japan itself.

5 🦘 Australia Tips

ラーメン屋を探すときの表現

"Do you know any good ramen places around here?" や "I'm craving ramen — any recommendations?" が自然です。craving は「〜がどうしても食べたい」という強い欲求を表します。"Is there anywhere that serves authentic Japanese ramen?" とauthenticを使うと本格的な店を探している意味になります。

間接話法のパターン

直接話法 "I spent years perfecting the recipe" は間接話法で "He is said to have spent years perfecting the recipe" となります。be said to + 完了不定詞は「〜したと言われている」の意味で、フォーマルな文章でよく見られるパターンです。

6 Conversation
🎭 メルボルンで美味しいラーメン屋を探している
A (Koa):
I'm absolutely craving ramen tonight. Do you know anywhere good?
B:
If it's ramen you're after, I know just the place. There's a shop on Russell Street that's supposed to be incredible.
A (Koa):
Really? What have you heard about it?
B:
A friend of mine, who lived in Japan for five years, said it was the closest thing to real Hakata ramen she'd found outside of Fukuoka.
A (Koa):
That's a strong recommendation! If she said that, it must be genuinely authentic.
B:
She also mentioned that the chef, having trained in Fukuoka for over a decade, uses only imported ingredients.
A (Koa):
I wish more restaurants took that approach. So many places cut corners with instant broth.
B:
Exactly. Were they to use shortcuts, a trained palate would notice immediately.
A (Koa):
Is it usually crowded? I'd rather not wait too long — I'm starving!
B:
It can be. Had we left earlier, we could have avoided the dinner rush. But it's worth the wait.
A (Koa):
Alright, let's go for it. I haven't had proper ramen since I left Tokyo.
B:
You won't be disappointed. Not only is the ramen outstanding, but the gyoza are also said to be the best in Melbourne.
