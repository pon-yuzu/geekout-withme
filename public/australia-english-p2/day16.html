Australia's Obsession with Brunch

Were a visitor from abroad to observe how Australians spend their weekends, they would quickly notice a phenomenon deeply embedded in the culture: brunch. Far more than a simple meal consumed between breakfast and lunch, brunch in Australia has evolved into a social ritual that reflects the nation's values of leisure and community. The Australian brunch scene, having been shaped by decades of multicultural immigration, offers an astonishing diversity of dishes. Avocado toast, which is widely regarded as having originated in Australian cafés during the 1990s, has since become a global sensation. It is estimated that Australians spend over four billion dollars annually on brunch-related dining. Were it not for the country's temperate climate, the outdoor dining culture that accompanies brunch might not have flourished so spectacularly. Patrons who wish to secure a table at popular venues are often advised that they arrive before ten o'clock, as queues extending down the street are not uncommon.

3 Review
📍 The Grounds of Alexandria — Sydney
⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

If there were one brunch destination in Sydney that I wished every visitor could experience, it would be The Grounds of Alexandria. Converted from a former industrial pie factory, this sprawling venue feels as though you had stepped into a countryside garden rather than an inner-city suburb. The eggs Benedict, served on house-baked sourdough with hollandaise sauce prepared from scratch, was exceptional. What impressed me most was the commitment to sustainability — the venue operates its own garden, from which many ingredients are sourced directly. The staff mentioned that had they not prioritised locally grown produce, the menu would lack its distinctive character. Surrounded by lush greenery and charming rustic décor, diners reported feeling as if they were on holiday rather than in the middle of Sydney. I was informed that weekend reservations should be made well in advance, as the venue attracts thousands of visitors each week. Rarely have I encountered a brunch experience so thoroughly enchanting.

5 🦘 Australia Tips

ブランチに誘うときの表現

"Do you feel like grabbing brunch this weekend?" や "I was wondering if you'd be up for brunch on Saturday" が自然な誘い方です。"Would you like to..." よりも "Do you feel like ...ing" の方がカジュアルで友達同士では好まれます。断るときは "I wish I could, but..." と仮定法を使うと丁寧です。

分詞構文で文を洗練させる

"Having been shaped by immigration, the menu is diverse."（移民に形作られてきたので、メニューは多様だ）のように、分詞構文は原因・理由を表す際に便利です。完了形の分詞 "having + 過去分詞" は主節の動作より前の出来事を示します。

6 Conversation
🎭 オーストラリア人の友達にブランチに誘われる
A (Koa):
Hey, I was wondering if you'd be keen to grab brunch tomorrow morning.
B:
I'd love to! Is there a particular place you had in mind?
A (Koa):
There's this café I've been meaning to try. It's said to have the best avocado toast in the city.
B:
Sounds amazing! I've heard that Australian cafés take brunch very seriously.
A (Koa):
They do! If you were to compare it to brunch in Japan, you'd notice some big differences.
B:
Like what? I'm curious to know what makes it so special here.
A (Koa):
Well, the portions are much larger, and the menus, having been influenced by so many cultures, are incredibly diverse.
B:
That sounds wonderful. What time should we meet? I was told the popular places get crowded early.
A (Koa):
You were told correctly! Were we to arrive after ten, we'd probably have to wait at least thirty minutes.
B:
In that case, shall we aim for nine? I'd rather not spend half the morning standing in a queue.
A (Koa):
Nine works perfectly. I wish the queues weren't so long, but it's a sign of how good the food is.
B:
Absolutely! I can't wait. Should I bring anything?
