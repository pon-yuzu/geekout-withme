Thriving in an Australian Commercial Kitchen

For many Working Holiday makers, kitchen work represents the most readily available form of employment, particularly in the bustling hospitality scenes of Sydney and Melbourne. A commercial kitchen, characterised by its fast pace and demanding environment, requires employees to be not only physically resilient but also capable of communicating efficiently under pressure. Were a new kitchen hand to neglect basic food safety protocols, the consequences could be severe, potentially resulting in the establishment being fined or shut down entirely. It is therefore essential that all kitchen staff undergo proper training before commencing their duties. Terminology specific to the culinary industry must be mastered promptly — expressions such as 'behind you,' uttered to prevent collisions, 'all day,' indicating the total quantity of a particular dish required, and 'eighty-six,' signifying that an item is no longer available, are used incessantly throughout service. Having familiarised themselves with these expressions, newcomers typically find that integration into the kitchen team accelerates considerably. The hierarchical structure, ranging from the head chef down to the kitchen hand, determines not only responsibilities but also the manner in which communication flows during service.

📍 Mama Rosa's Italian Kitchen, Lygon Street, Melbourne
⭐⭐⭐⭐

Working at Mama Rosa's for three months was, without question, one of the most formative experiences of my time in Australia. Never before had I worked in such an intense environment, where every second counted and precision was paramount. The head chef, a passionate Italian woman whose culinary expertise was truly extraordinary, insisted that every dish leaving the kitchen meet her exacting standards. It was she who taught me that the expression 'behind you' could quite literally prevent serious burns and injuries, a lesson I wish I had learned before accidentally colliding with a colleague carrying a pot of boiling water on my second day. Having gradually adapted to the rhythm of the kitchen, I found myself genuinely enjoying the adrenaline of a Friday night service, during which we would serve upwards of three hundred covers. Were anyone to ask me whether kitchen work is worth the physical demands, I would respond without hesitation that the skills acquired, the friendships forged, and the resilience developed make it an invaluable experience.

5 🦘 Australia Tips

キッチンの掛け声を覚えよう

'Behind!' は最も重要なキッチン用語です。誰かの後ろを通る時に必ず言います。他にも 'Corner!'（角を曲がる時）、'Hot!'（熱いものを運ぶ時）、'Sharp!'（刃物を持っている時）があります。これらは安全のために不可欠な表現です。

分詞構文で原因・結果を表現

'Having familiarised themselves with these expressions, newcomers find integration easier' のように、分詞構文は原因や時間の前後関係を簡潔に表せます。キッチンの忙しい場面を描写する時にも 'Feeling overwhelmed, I took a deep breath' のように使えます。

6 Conversation
🎭 キッチンでの初日、先輩スタッフに仕事を教わる場面
A (Koa):
Right, welcome to the kitchen. It can get pretty hectic in here, so there are a few things you need to know from the get-go.
B:
I'm ready to learn. I've never worked in a commercial kitchen before, so I'd appreciate any guidance you can offer.
A (Koa):
No worries. First things first — whenever you're walking behind someone, you must call out 'behind.' Were you to forget, you could cause a serious accident.
B:
Understood. I've read about that. What about the term 'all day'? I heard the chef say it earlier.
A (Koa):
Good question. When the chef says 'three steaks all day,' it means three steaks are needed in total across all current orders. It's essential that you grasp this distinction.
B:
Got it. And if we run out of something, what's the protocol?
A (Koa):
You'd call out 'eighty-six' followed by the item. For instance, 'Eighty-six the barramundi.' That tells the front of house to stop taking orders for that dish.
B:
That's fascinating. I wish I had studied these terms before starting. What should I focus on during my first shift?
A (Koa):
Primarily, you'll be doing prep work — chopping vegetables, portioning ingredients, and keeping the workspace spotless. The chef insists that the kitchen be immaculate at all times.
B:
I'll make sure of that. Is there anything else I should be mindful of?
A (Koa):
Always announce when you're carrying something hot or sharp. And never, under any circumstances, run in the kitchen. Safety comes first, no matter how rushed service gets.
B:
Absolutely. I'm genuinely excited to be part of this team. Thank you for being so thorough with the explanation.
