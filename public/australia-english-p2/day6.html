1 Reading

先住民文化

The World's Oldest Living Culture

The Indigenous peoples of Australia, comprising both Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander communities, possess what is widely regarded as the oldest continuously surviving culture in human history, spanning an estimated 65,000 years. Prior to European colonisation in 1788, it is believed that approximately 750,000 Indigenous people inhabited the continent, speaking over 250 distinct languages. Their sophisticated understanding of the natural environment, refined over millennia, encompassed intricate knowledge of seasonal patterns, medicinal plants, and sustainable land management techniques — including the practice of controlled burning, which scientists now recognise as an ecologically beneficial approach to preventing catastrophic bushfires. The Dreamtime, or Dreaming, constitutes the foundational spiritual framework through which Indigenous Australians interpret the creation of the land, its creatures, and the laws governing human conduct. Not only do these narratives serve a spiritual purpose, but they also function as practical guides for navigating the landscape. Regrettably, European settlement inflicted devastating consequences upon Indigenous communities, the effects of which continue to reverberate through Australian society to this day.

3 Review
📍 National Museum of Australia, Canberra
⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

Located on the shores of Lake Burley Griffin in Canberra, the National Museum of Australia offers what is arguably the most comprehensive and respectful presentation of Indigenous Australian history available anywhere in the country. Having dedicated an entire afternoon to the First Australians gallery, I emerged with a profoundly deeper understanding of the richness and complexity of Aboriginal culture. What particularly moved me was the exhibit on the Stolen Generations — the period during which Indigenous children were forcibly removed from their families by government authorities, a practice that persisted well into the twentieth century. The museum handles this confronting subject matter with remarkable sensitivity, allowing visitors to hear directly from survivors through recorded testimonies. Not only does the museum document the historical injustices suffered by Indigenous peoples, but it also celebrates their extraordinary resilience and the vibrant continuation of their cultural traditions. Whoever visits this museum will come away with an appreciation of why reconciliation remains one of the most significant ongoing conversations in contemporary Australian society.

5 🦘 Australia Tips

先住民文化への敬意

オーストラリアでは公式イベントの冒頭に「Acknowledgement of Country」（その土地の伝統的所有者への敬意表明）が行われます。ウルルやカカドゥなど聖地を訪れる際は、撮影禁止エリアを厳守しましょう。先住民アートを購入する際は、先住民アーティストが直接利益を得られる正規のギャラリーを選ぶことが重要です。

ワーホリで体験できる先住民文化

ウルルのドットペインティング体験、ケアンズ近郊のジャプカイ・アボリジナル文化パーク、シドニーのアボリジナル・ヘリテージ・ツアーなどがおすすめです。ブーメラン投げやディジュリドゥの演奏体験ができるツアーもあります。先住民ガイドによるブッシュタッカー（伝統的な食べ物）ツアーは、自然への深い知識に感動する体験です。

6 Conversation
🎭 ユウキが、大学の先生であるオーストラリア人のトム先生と先住民文化について話しています。
A (Koa):
Today I'd like to discuss Indigenous Australian culture. Yuuki, what do you already know about it?
B:
I've read that it's considered the oldest continuously surviving culture in the world, spanning approximately 65,000 years. That's truly remarkable.
A (Koa):
It absolutely is. To put that in perspective, that's roughly ten times longer than recorded human civilisation. Prior to European arrival, there were over 250 distinct languages spoken across the continent.
B:
I had no idea there were so many languages! How many of those have survived to the present day?
A (Koa):
Sadly, only about 120 are still spoken, and many of those are critically endangered. Were it not for dedicated language preservation programmes, even fewer would have survived.
B:
That's deeply concerning. I've also learned about the Dreamtime. Could you explain what that represents?
A (Koa):
The Dreamtime, or Dreaming, is the spiritual framework through which Indigenous Australians understand the creation of everything — the land, animals, plants, and the laws that govern society. It's not merely a set of myths; it's a living, breathing cultural system that continues to guide daily life.
B:
That's fascinating. In Japan, we have Shinto beliefs about nature spirits, so I can somewhat relate to that worldview.
A (Koa):
That's a perceptive comparison. Another aspect worth noting is fire-stick farming — the practice of controlled burning that Indigenous Australians have employed for thousands of years to manage the landscape.
B:
I've heard that modern scientists now recognise the ecological benefits of that practice. Is that correct?
A (Koa):
Precisely. It reduces fuel loads and prevents catastrophic bushfires. Had European settlers adopted Indigenous land management practices earlier, the severity of recent bushfires might have been considerably reduced.
B:
It's impressive that such sophisticated environmental knowledge existed long before modern science. I would love to experience some aspects of Indigenous culture during my time here.
